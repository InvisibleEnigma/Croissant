🥐 Croissants from the Fakery

The non-existent bakery

The croissant, often seen as a classic French pastry, actually has its roots in Austria. Austrian bakers in the 13th century crafted a crescent-shaped pastry known as the "kipferl." By the 19th century, this pastry made its way to France, where bakers transformed it into the croissant we recognise today. French bakers used laminated dough, layering it with butter to achieve the delicate, flaky texture that defines the modern croissant. Making croissants involves a meticulous process called laminating. This technique requires folding butter into dough multiple times, creating thin layers that result in the pastry’s light, airy structure and crisp crust. After shaping the dough into its iconic crescent, it’s left to proof, allowing the flavours to develop and the pastry to rise. Finally, it’s baked to golden perfection, showcasing the rich layers and buttery taste that have made it loved worldwide
